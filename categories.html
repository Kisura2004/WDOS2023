Turtle categories

1.Green Turtle (Chelonia mydas)

The largest hard-shelled sea turtle is the green sea turtle. they mostly consume seagrasses and algae as herbivores. Their name derives from the greenish hue of their fat, not from the color of their shells. There are green turtles all around the world. They have nesting grounds in more than 80 countries and live around the coastlines of over 140 countries. Green turtles have historically hunted for their meat, eggs, and fat, which has led to a drop in global green turtle population. The killing of sea turtles and the taking of their eggs are illegal in many countries, but still in some areas killing of green turtles for resources remains a threat for them.

2. Loggerhead (Caretta caretta)

The loggerhead turtle is named for its large head, which supports powerful jaw muscles that enable them to feed on hard-shelled prey, such as whelks and conch. Loggerheads are the most abundant species of sea turtle that nests in the United States. Juvenile and adult loggerheads live in U.S. coastal waters, but many adults that nest on U.S. beaches migrate from neighboring nations like the Bahamas, Cuba, and Mexico. Loggerhead populations in the United States declined due to bycatch in fishing gear such as trawls, gillnets, and longlines.

3. Leatherback (Dermochelys coriacea)

The leatherback sea turtle is the largest turtle in the world. They are the only species of sea turtle that lack scales and a hard shell. They are named for their tough rubbery skin and have existed in their current form since the age of the dinosaurs. Leatherbacks are highly migratory, some swimming over 10,000 miles a year between nesting and foraging grounds reaching different parts of the world while spreading there habitats. They are also accomplished divers with the deepest recorded dive reaching nearly 4,000 feet—deeper than most marine mammals.

4. Olive Ridley (Lepidochelys olivacea)

The olive ridley gets its name from the olive green color of its heart-shaped shell. The species is among the smallest of the world’s sea turtles and is found primarily in the tropical regions of the Pacific, Indian, and Atlantic oceans. Olive ridley turtles are found throughout the world. The number of olive ridleys are greatly reduced from historical estimates due to overexploitation for turtle meat and eggs

5. Hawksbill (Eretmochelys imbricata)

hawksbill turtles can be found in tropical and subtropical waters of all major oceans in the world. The name "hawksbill" refers to the unusual beak-like mouth, which is ideal for finding food sources in narrow cracks and crevices. They are the only type of sea turtle that can survive only on sponges.  Hawksbills are threatened by hunting in many regions of the world because of their unique shell, often known as "tortoise shell," which is used by craftsmens to make different jewelleries and ornaments. Hawksbills were historically hunted and killed for its shell, almost causing the extinction of the species.
